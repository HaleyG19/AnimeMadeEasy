Ok, three, two, one, let's jam! (1980 - 1989)

The 1980s are often regarded as the "golden age" of anime because it's at this time that the medium really took off and established an international fan base.

Riding off the success of the previous generation, the 1980s were a fantastic decade for the anime industry. With the help of VHS and other home video devices, anime became more accessible than ever and technological advancements like CGI made Japanese animation look better than ever before allowing studios to increase budgets as profits flowed in.

Genres that had previously never been explored were now becoming popular in the industry. For instance, the sports anime genre really gained speed with the 1983 television series Captain Tsubasa which promoted themes of friendship, teamwork, and kick-ass sports moves.

Captain Tusbasa, 1983

Likewise, with the success of American films like Star Wars (1977), space operas became another popular anime genre leading to the creation of series such as Macross in 1982 and sequals to the Mobile Suit Gundam series in 1985. As well, anime such as Dragon Ball, (1986) further expanded the action genre and would go on to influence many hit series in the years to come.

Dragon Ball, 1986

Coinciding with the rise of these great anime, a new subculture of Otaku emerged. Otaku is the Japanese word used to describe someone who is particularly intense or obsessive with certain parts of popular culture, like anime. This subculture drove the creation of many anime titles and led some studios to cater specifically to their interests leading to the creation of a lot of weird shit.

Finally, the 1980s also witnessed the founding of one of the biggest and most influential anime studios of all time; Studio Ghibli, in 1985. The studio was founded by Hayao Miyazai, who at the time was already famous for directing the highly successful film Nausica of the Valley of the Wind (Kaze no Tani no Naushika) in 1984. Along with co-founder Isao Takahata, Miyazayki and Studio Ghibli would go on to make some of the decades greatest anime films like Laputa: Castle in the Sky (Tenku no Shiro Rapyuta) in 1986 and Kiki's Delivery Service (Majo no Takkyubin) in 1989.

Nausica of the Valley of the Wind (Kaze no Tani no Naushika), 1984

And of course, discussion of this golden decade would not be complete without mentioning Akira. Bringing the decade to a close, Katsuhiro Otomo's anime film Akira was the most expensive film of its time and is largely responsible for promoting the industry's fan base internationally even though it was a major flop in Japan.

Akira, 1989
