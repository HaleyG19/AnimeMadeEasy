The Second World War and Beyond (1939 - 1959)

Despite the destruction and economic struggles of this period, the anime industry began to take shape and thrive.

In the late 1930s, the Japanese government began to enforce an agenda of cultural nationalism which censored and controlled all published media. Although initially this sounds like it would be a drawback, it actually helped the anime industry get back on its feet as creators were now urged to produce content that aligned with the Japanese spirit and national affiliation.

Many studios took advantage of this newfound support and began making films for the Japanese government and military. Promotional videos and propaganda films flourished at this time, especially during the war, and advertisements and educational films for children were also popular and remain common uses of anime today.

In 1945, with the funding of the Japanese navy, Mitsuyo Seo created the first feature-length anime film entitled Momotaro's Divine Sea Warriors (Momotaro: Umi no Shinpei). The film is black and white and tells the story of an anthropomorphic monkey and dog and their time in the Japanese military helping primitive islanders and invading British land. Although at first the film seems to be just another propaganda piece, many have said they were moved by the hidden underlying message of hope for peace.

Momotaro's Divine Sea Warriors (Momotaro: Umi no Shinpei), 1945

In 1948, the first modern Japanese production company was founded by Kenzo Masaoka and Zenjiro Yamamoto and called Toei Animation. This studio produced the first colour anime feature-film called The Tale of the White Serpent (Hakujaden) in 1958 which enjoyed success both in Japan and abroad. With this film Toei Animation developed what has become known as the "money shot" which is a scene animated with more detail than others to emphasize importance, and this helped cut down on the costs of animation exponentially.

The Tale of the White Serpent (Hakujaden), 1958
